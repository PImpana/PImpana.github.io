London Crime Analysis Project

Aims of the Project: Investigating the crime rates across socio economic regions in London

Safety and security are essential responsibilities that every government must fulfill. This project seeks to examine crime patterns across London's boroughs, observing any regional differences. It aims to connect crime data with socio-economic indicators. The main objective is to present clear, evidence-based findings to support thoughtful and effective policymaking.

The area chart below illustrates the types of crimes committed in London in 2024. The most common offences were theft and violence against the person. Median Yearly pay across Boroughs

This bar chart shows the annual median pay across London boroughs, averaged from 2013 to 2024, alongside the national average. According to the chart, Tower hamlets has the highest median earnings, followed by Westminster. In contrast, Redbridge and Harrow have the lowest median pay, falling below the national average.

Median Annual Pay (Â£) of Borough by Total Crime

This scatter plot explores the relationship between total reported crimes and median annual pay across various London boroughs. Interestingly, some of the boroughs with the highest median pay, such as Tower Hamlets and Westminster, report higher crime rates, challenging the assumption that higher crime rates are typically found in lower-income areas. However, this could reflect the influence of wealthier populations and the types of crimes occurring in these regions. Conversely, Harrow, one of the boroughs with the lowest median pay, reports relatively fewer crimes.

We fit a best fit line into the total reported crimes and median annual pay across the London boroughs. The R square value for the regression is 0.31 which means that approximately 31% of the change in the total crimes can be explained by the median pay. Although there is a general trend there could be various other factors that affect the increase or decrease in crimes and hence we fail to establish a causal relationship. The general trend shows with the increase in median pay, crimes tend to increase as well.

Data

The data used in the visualisation of the charts were sourced from two main data repositories.

1.Office of National Statistics (ONS): The data on total reported crimes,borough wise crime data, and median pay was derived from ONS.

2.Metropolitan Police Service:The data on the level of crimes committed by borough was accessed through Metropolitan Police Service website. The Metropolitan API was used as well.

The datasets used to perform the analysis is accesible via GitHub repository. The data processing and visualization were performed using Python, and vegalite.

Analytical Challenges

The project presented several analytical challenges. Chart 2 had x-axis formatting issues in Vega-Lite, which I resolved using data transformation techniques from class. A common issue was converting data from wide to long format, addressed by restructuring the data with Python. Combining multiple data sources, especially for Chart 5, where yearly pay and total crimes were plotted with bubbles, caused overlapping. I resolved this by adding a tooltip feature for better clarity and interactivity. These challenges were overcome using solutions from class, including pandas, Vega-Lite, and a strong understanding of the datasets.

Conclusion

The analysis shows theft and violence against individuals are the most common types of crimes reported. However, a closer look reveals that crime rates vary significantly across different boroughs. We examined the relationship between median earnings and crime rates in each borough and identified a trend where higher-income areas tend to have higher crime rates. Westminster, a high-income borough, recorded the highest number of reported crimes, with theft being the predominant offense. This suggests that higher-income boroughs experiences higher crime rates due to the prevalence of theft implying that these areas must have an increase in surveillance and safety measures.
